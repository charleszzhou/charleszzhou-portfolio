Sampling Frequency Effects in DC Motor Control

Experimental Analysis of Discrete-Time PI Control

Executive Summary

Experimentally investigated how controller sampling frequency influences closed-loop DC motor speed regulation. Using a discrete-time PI controller, motor response was evaluated across a wide range of sample rates to quantify effects on tracking accuracy, transient response, and stability.

Problem & Purpose

In digital control systems, the choice of sampling frequency directly affects stability margins and achievable performance. This project quantifies how discrete sampling rates—from low (~10 Hz) to high (~100 Hz)—impact RMS tracking error, overshoot, and settling behavior in a DC motor speed control task.

Experimental Setup

  • Controller: Discrete-time PI controller implemented on a microcontroller.
  • Plant: DC motor with quadrature encoder providing velocity feedback.
  • Sampling rates: Discrete loop frequencies from 10 Hz to 100 Hz.
  • Test input: Step command from 0 → 4 rad/s with high-rate data logging.

Key Results

  • Tracking accuracy: RMS tracking error decreases monotonically with increasing sampling frequency.
  • Low-rate behavior: Sampling below ~20 Hz introduces oscillations and sluggish response.
  • Diminishing returns: Performance gains taper beyond ~50 Hz, indicating a practical bandwidth limit.
  • Transient response: Higher sampling rates reduce overshoot and variability in settling time.

Engineering Insight

The results highlight a key tradeoff in embedded control: while increasing sampling frequency improves performance, gains are ultimately limited by plant dynamics, sensor resolution, and computation constraints. Selecting an appropriate loop rate is therefore a system-level design decision, not simply a matter of maximizing frequency.
